Galaxies contain a mixture of gas, dust, and stars in perpetual interactions. Under the action of gravity, the gas is converted into stars which produce energy and convert light elements (hydrogen, helium) into heavier elements ("metals": carbon, oxygen,...). Stars then enrich the gas by re-injecting metals that are the basis of dust grains, where chemistry can take place.
Galaxies are evolving. They form stars by consuming their gas reservoir ("blue star-forming galaxies"). Once they have consumed their gas reservoir, star formation stopped and they are called "red and dead". However, star formation is complex and some processes can oppose gravity.
My research focused on star formation in nearby galaxies. I tried to understand the processes at play in star formation, and the effect of the environment on the gas/star formation relation. In particular, I was studying the so-called "AGN feedback" and "jet-induced star formation" (more details below). I used data science method to analyse and interpret astronomical observations, mainly based on radio astronomy.

AGN feedback

Galaxies host a supermassive black hole (SMBH; at least 100 000 times the mass of the Sun)) in their centre. Around a SMBH, matter is forming an accretion disc which emits light under the influence of the SMBH. When the emitted light from the accretion disc reaches a certain limit, astrophysicists talk about a "Active Galactic Nucleus" (AGN; pl. Nuclei). In addition to light, AGN can also produce jets of particles that is seen in radio emission.
AGN are among the most powerful and luminous objects in the Universe and are playing a role in the evolution of galaxies. In particular, they are thought to regulate star formation in the most massive galaxies: the energy they produce can heat or remove the gas of the host galaxy ("AGN feedback"). AGN can also locally favour star formation bycompressing the gas ("positive feedback"). This is the case in few radio galaxies where the radio jets ignites the star formation ("jet-induced star formation").

Centaurus A

Centaurus A is the most nearby powerful AGN and the largest radio source from outside the Milky Way. The AGN produces large radio jet that extend up to about 250 kpc (the parsec is a unit of distance used by astrophysicists, equal to 3.26 light-year; for instance the Milky Way has a diameter of 30 kpc).
NGC 5128, the galaxy that hosts Centaurus A, is surrounded by faint arc-like shells of stars and atomic gas, at a radius of several kpc around the galaxy. At the intersection of the radio jet with one shell of gas, bright star-forming filaments are observed, suggesting that star formation is triggered by the radio jet. Because Centaurus A is really close, it is the ideal object to study jet-induced star formation.

Radio astronomy

Radio astronomy is a branch of observational astronomy that observe the Universe with radio waves at frequencies from few MHz to hundreds of GHz. At this frequencies, the atmosphere is transparent and has little effects on the observations (on the opposite, the atmosphere blocks most of the ultraviolet or infrared light and satellites are necessary to make observations).
Radio observations are conducted using large radio antennas (like the Arecibo Telescope where the climactic fight of the movie GoldenEye takes place). To reach high resolutions, several radio antennas are connected and observe simultaneously the same object, forming a radio interferometer (like the Very Large Array in the movie Contact).
Two modes of observations exist, depending on the origin of the radio emission: the "continuum" mode observe in a large range of frequencies, while the "line" mode samples the emission into smaller frequency channels. Within a gas, the molecules and atoms emit light at given frequencies or wavelengths (both terms are interchangeable but one is most commonly used, depending on the region of the spectrum). Each molecule and atom has its own spectral "signature". Therefore, to observe and study the gas within and around galaxies, astrophysicists use the line mode.
